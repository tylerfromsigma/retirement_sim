New to Monte Carlo simulations? Start here.
▾

Nobody knows what the stock market will do over the next 30 years — so instead of guessing, this tool replays history thousands of times using real returns from 1926 to today. Each replay (a "simulation") strings together a random sequence of past years — booms, crashes, inflation spikes, all of it — and rolls your portfolio forward, withdrawing your spending along the way.

1 · Setup
You enter your portfolio, spending, age, and asset allocation in the sidebar.
2 · Simulate
We run ~1,000 alternate futures, each using a different random sequence of real historical market years.
3 · Summarize
We report the range of outcomes — not a single forecast.

The headline number is the success rate: the percentage of those simulations where your money outlasted you. A 90% success rate means that out of every 100 plausible market histories, your plan survived in 90 of them.

The percentile bands show the spread of outcomes. If markets are kind to you (the 95th percentile), you'll end up with much more than the median. If they're cruel — especially in your first few retirement years (sequence-of-returns risk) — you could end up at the 5th percentile with much less, or broke. This honest range is more useful than any single "you'll have $X at 90" prediction, because retirement planning is fundamentally about probabilities, not certainties.

Portfolio Value Over Time — Percentile Bands iEach line or shaded band tracks your portfolio balance year by year. The wide outer band covers 80% of all outcomes (10th–90th percentile); the narrower inner band covers 50% (25th–75th). The blue line is the median — half of all simulations end above it, half below. Red lines are sample runs that failed. The fan shape widens over time because small early differences compound dramatically — this is sequence-of-returns risk in action.
